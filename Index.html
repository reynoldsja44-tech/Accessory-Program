Chin Tuck + Neck NodDeep cervical flexors · Craniocervical alignment
2 × 12 reps
Supine (lying on back)
▶
Lie on your back, knees bent. Draw your chin straight back ("double chin"), then slowly nod your head yes — lifting only the top of your skull. Directly strengthens the deep stabilizers weakened by forward head posture.
  • Chin tuck is a glide backward, not a crunch downward
  • The nod range is tiny — 10–15° at most
  • Think: lengthen the back of your neck, not shorten the front

Isometric Neck Holds (4 Directions)Deep cervical flexors · SCM · Extensors · Lateral flexors
3 × 10 sec each
Hand vs. head
▶
Press your palm against your head and resist with your neck — no movement. Four directions: forward (chin to chest resist), backward, and both sides (ear to shoulder resist).
  • ~70% of max effort — hard work, not a fight
  • Keep jaw relaxed, breathing steady
  • Forward direction targets the most neglected neck muscles
  • Progress to 20-second holds over weeks

3 × 10 reps
Arms against wall
▶
Stand with back, arms, and hands flat against a wall. Slide arms overhead while keeping full contact — back of hands, forearms, elbows. Slide back down.

  • If contact breaks, only go as high as you can maintain it
  • Squeeze lower traps at the top, not upper traps

▶
Sit with both legs at 90° angles. Rotate hips to switch sides, sweeping both legs through. Torso upright throughout.

  • Hands lightly on floor for balance only

Feet flat if possible
▶
Drop into a deep squat and hold. Use a doorframe for support if needed. While in the squat, rotate your torso, press knees out with elbows, explore positions.

  • Chest up, spine long
  • Use the hold time actively — shift weight side to side

Standing Hip CARsHip capsule · Full circumduction pattern
3 reps / side
One hand on wall
▶
Stand on one leg. Lift the other and trace the largest possible circle with the knee — forward, out, behind, back. Keep the standing hip from shifting.
  • Maximum range, not speed
  • Pelvis level — don't let it hike or drop
  • Reverse direction for equal reps

💪 Glute Med Superset — Clamshells + Side Raises ~10 min
ClamshellsGluteus medius · Gluteus minimus · External hip rotators
3 × 20 / side
Band above knees (optional)
▶
Lie on your side, hips stacked, knees bent ~45°. Keeping feet together, rotate top knee upward like a clamshell opening. 1–2 second hold at top, lower slowly. Do all reps on one side, immediately follow with side raises, then flip.
  • Pelvis completely still — if your hips roll back, you've gone too far
  • Movement from the hip, not from arching your back
  • Band above knees adds intensity significantly
  • Weak glute med is a primary cause of IT band syndrome and runner's knee
IT band / runner's knee preventionRunning lateral stabilityHip drop prevention
Side-Lying Leg RaisesGluteus medius · TFL · Hip abductors (full range)
3 × 15 / side
Slow controlled lower
▶
Lie on your side, body in a straight line (hips stacked, not bent). Top leg straight, raise it ~45° then lower slowly. Do these immediately after clamshells on the same side before flipping.
  • Toes slightly toward the floor (internal rotation) isolates glute med over TFL
  • Don't let the top hip hike upward as leg rises
  • 1-second pause at the top
  • Progress: ankle weight or band above ankles
Lateral knee / hip stabilityVolleyball lateral shuffle powerRunning pelvic control

▶
Band around a fixed point, step into it behind the knee. From slight bend, fully straighten the knee against the band resistance, squeezing the quad at lockout.

  • Don't hyperextend at lockout

3-second lower
▶
Stand on a step. Slowly lower the opposite foot toward the ground by bending the standing knee — 3 seconds down. Return.

Against wall or couch

Active recovery day — slower pace, longer holds. No heavy resistance. The goal is nervous system work: proprioception, thoracic mobility, and core stability. Hold each position slightly longer than feels comfortable.
⚖️ Balance & Proprioception ~12 min
Single-Leg RDL Balance HoldPosterior chain · Proprioception · Hip hinge
3 × 25 sec / side
Eyes open → eyes closed
▶
Stand on one leg, hinge forward at the hip (RDL position), back leg floats up. Hold still. Progress: close your eyes.
  • Spine neutral, not rounded
  • Standing foot grips floor like a tripod
  • Eyes closed version is 3× harder — that's normal
  • Most important balance exercise for injury prevention across all your sports

Hip Flexor Lift-Off (Active Psoas)Psoas · Iliacus · Hip flexion strength
3 × 10 / side
Seated at bench edge
▶
Sit at the edge of a bench. Actively lift one knee toward your chest as high as possible while keeping torso upright. Hold 2 seconds at the top.
  • Don't lean back as the knee rises — stay tall
  • Pull from the hip crease, not the knee
  • Progress: small weight on the thigh
  • Direct antidote to hip flexor inhibition from prolonged cycling

Seated, foot elevated

Seated, leg extended. Slowly trace circles (5 each direction), then spell the alphabet with your big toe as the pen.

  • Great to do first thing in the morning, even still in bed

🌀 Core & Thoracic ~13 min
Dead Bug (Contralateral)Transverse abdominis · Multifidus · Lumbar stability
3 × 8 / side
Low back pressed flat
▶
Lie on back, arms to ceiling, knees at 90° over hips. Simultaneously lower opposite arm and leg toward floor, keeping lower back flat. Return. Alternate.
  • Press lower back into floor the entire time — no gap
  • Move slowly enough that the back stays engaged
  • Exhale as limbs lower; inhale as they return
  • If lower back lifts, reduce the range — don't push through

Bear Plank Hold + Shoulder TapCore anti-rotation · Shoulder stability · Wrist loading
3 × 10 taps / side
Hips level and still
▶
Bear plank (knees 2 inches off floor, back flat). Lift one hand, tap opposite shoulder, hips perfectly still. Return. Alternate.
  • Widen feet to reduce hip rotation, then narrow as you improve
  • Zero hip wobble — slow down to achieve it
  • Also loads wrists progressively

Standing Thoracic RotationThoracic spine · Rib cage mobility · Rotational range
3 × 10 / side
Arms crossed at chest
▶
Stand, feet hip-width, slight knee bend, arms crossed at chest. Rotate upper body as far as possible to one side while keeping hips forward. Pause at end range.
  • Hips stay square — rotation is purely thoracic
  • Look over your shoulder to maximize the rotation
  • Exhale at end range to gain a few more degrees
  • Counter-clockwise is often tighter in right-handed athletes

Seated or standing
▶
Toe yoga: lift only big toe keeping others down, then reverse. Short foot: without curling toes, dome your arch by pulling the ball of your foot toward your heel.

  • Short foot: imagine a string pulling the ball of your foot toward your heel
  • Progress: hold short foot while performing calf raises

The strength-focused upper day. Where Monday was mobility-first, today leads with wrist and forearm loading. Pairs well before a lift or volleyball session. The wrist roller goes last — it's a finisher.
🤝 Wrist & Forearm Strength ~14 min
Wrist Curls + Reverse CurlsFlexor carpi · Wrist extensors · Forearm strength
3 × 15 each
Light DB or water bottle
▶
Forearm resting on thigh, hand hanging off edge. Wrist curl: palm up, curl weight upward. Reverse curl: flip to palm down, curl upward by extending the wrist.
  • Use very light weight — 1–3 kg is plenty
  • Full range — let wrist drop all the way before curling
  • Slow and deliberate — precision work, not a race
  • Reverse curls target the extensors — key for tennis elbow prevention
Tennis elbow preventionGrip enduranceVolleyball setting / blocking
Forearm Rotation with Offset LoadPronator teres · Supinator · Radioulnar strength
3 × 15 each direction
DB held at one end
▶
Hold a dumbbell at one end (or a hammer). Elbow bent at 90°. Slowly rotate palm up (supination), then palm down (pronation). The offset load creates torque that directly loads the rotators.
  • Elbow pinned to your side — movement is purely in the forearm
  • A longer, lighter object creates more leverage — a hammer is ideal
  • Control through the entire arc — don't let gravity do the work
  • Protects against cycling handlebar grip stress

Wrist Roller — Roll-Up / Roll-DownFull forearm complex · Grip endurance · Wrist stability under load
3 × full roll each way
Arms at sides or extended
▶
Use a wrist roller or DIY (short stick, rope, light weight). Wind the rope up by alternating wrist curls, then slowly reverse to unwind.
  • Arms extended is harder — start with arms at sides if needed
  • Go slow on the unwind — eccentric phase is where adaptation happens
  • Start very light (0.5–1 kg) — it feels easy until suddenly it doesn't
  • DIY: tie a 500g weight to a thick marker with a shoelace
  • One of the most complete forearm exercises available

This split is a template, not a rigid prescription — shift days to match your training schedule. Upper A (Mon) and Upper B (Fri) run twice per week so the neck, shoulder, and wrist work gets two doses. Lower A hits the glutes and knees hard; Lower B targets the ankle-foot complex and hamstrings. Wednesday's recovery day is intentionally low-load — resist the urge to push harder on it. If you miss a day, don't double up; just continue from where you are.
